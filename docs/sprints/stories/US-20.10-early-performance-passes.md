## Goal

Hold the performance work that **shipped before the current programme started** — the first
optimization pass, the data-subscription review that followed the UI upgrade, the
extension–web-runner flow, the zkAssets removal from the runner bundle, a general WebApp pass, and
one API defect on the cached-gas endpoint. Six issues, all settled.

## Status

> **✅ done — every row below is settled**, six delivered across releases 1.0.5 → 1.0.7 and later
> closes with no release of their own. It carries **no FR**: the performance NFRs are defended by
> the forward stories in this epic, and this one records what already landed.
>
> **`version_shipped: 1.0.7` is a representative anchor, not the whole set** — the most recent
> constituent with a provable release, the same convention
> [US-9.13](US-9.13-nft-media-and-ipfs-gateway-pipeline.md) uses. Four of the six carry no release
> at all; the table is the full record.

## Scope

This story exists because of [AGENTS.md](../../../AGENTS.md) rule 9. When the performance ledger
was folded on 2026-07-22, these six settled issues landed in
[US-20.1](US-20.1-core-structure-and-lifecycle-refactor.md),
[US-20.2](US-20.2-api-call-optimization.md) and
[US-20.6](US-20.6-webapp-and-web-runner-performance.md) — three stories whose own scope is entirely
**open**. A table holding both is the mix rule 9 forbids, and the first attempt at fixing it added a
*second* table to each story to keep scope and history apart. That was a way around the rule, not a
way to keep it: **one story, one table, one completion state.**

So the settled half is here and the three parents keep only what they exist to deliver.

**Why one story rather than three.** Splitting six historical issues across three forward stories
would produce three near-empty `done` stories whose only content is other stories' prehistory. They
are one thing — *performance work that predates the programme* — and they read as one.
